Is that God speaking—or just anxiety? If you’ve ever stared at the ceiling at 2 a.m., wondering whether that intense thought is guidance or just your nerves, this is for you. Today, we’re walking through five simple, biblical tests to help you discern His voice with confidence.

Before we start, breathe. You’re not broken because you struggle with this. You love God, you want to obey Him, and that’s beautiful. The Bible says, My sheep hear My voice in John 10:27. God isn’t playing hide and seek with you. He’s a Shepherd who delights to lead, not a tyrant who delights to confuse. Think of today’s video as a calm, repeatable checklist for when your mind is loud and your heart wants clarity.

First, the Shepherd Test. John 10:27 reminds us that the voice we follow is a Person’s voice—Jesus. So ask, does this thought match the character of Christ? The voice of Jesus is truthful, loving, holy, and hopeful. He corrects, yes, but He never condemns His children. He invites repentance without stripping away your dignity. Anxiety often speaks in harsh, shaming, all-or-nothing language. It says, You always fail. God’s voice says, Turn back to Me. Let’s make this right. Anxiety barks panic, Jesus brings steady conviction that moves you toward life. If the inner message pushes you into fear, frenzy, and self-loathing, it fails the Shepherd Test. If it draws you to humility, love, and trust, even when it’s hard, you’re likely hearing the Shepherd.

Second, the Scripture Test. Second Timothy 3:16 says all Scripture is God-breathed and useful. God will never guide you in a way that contradicts His Word. He won’t tell you to lie to protect yourself, to mistreat someone made in His image, or to chase a shortcut that bypasses integrity. Sometimes we want a specific blueprint—take this job, move to that city—while Scripture gives us enduring principles: walk in truth, seek wisdom, love your neighbor, flee sexual immorality, do justice, love mercy, walk humbly. Here’s a gentle caution from psychology: confirmation bias is real. Our brains tend to find what we want to see, even in Scripture. That’s why context matters. Don’t isolate a verse to force an answer. Read around it. Let the Word correct you, not just confirm you. If the thought stands firm under the weight of Scripture—its context, its character, its cross-centered story—it passes the test.

Third, the Fruit and Peace Test. James 3:17 says wisdom from above is pure, peaceable, gentle, willing to yield, full of mercy and good fruits. Colossians 3:15 says let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts. The peace of Christ is not the same as zero nerves. You can feel butterflies and still have a deep, clean sense of rightness. What grows in you when you sit with this supposed guidance? Purity or compromise? Gentleness or harshness? Mercy or resentment? Clarity or compulsive dread? Anxiety often masquerades as urgency and control. God’s wisdom produces a peace that can coexist with trembling hands. Think of Jesus in Gethsemane: honest anguish, yet surrendered peace. If the fruit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control, you’re tasting wisdom from above. If the fruit is fear-driven control or impurity, pause.

Fourth, the Community Test. Proverbs 11:14 says where there is no guidance, a people falls, but in an abundance of counselors there is safety. God made us a body for a reason. Bring your sense of direction to wise, biblically grounded people who know you. You’re not outsourcing obedience; you’re inviting confirmation and correction. Psychology affirms this too: when thoughts stay in our heads, distortions grow. Speaking them aloud and getting feedback can shrink anxiety’s power and expose blind spots. If two or three mature believers consistently sense alignment with Scripture and the Spirit, pay attention. If the response is concern, defensiveness, or isolation on your part, slow down. God often humbles our pace through counsel, not to punish us, but to protect us.

Fifth, the Time Test. Habakkuk 2:3 says the vision awaits its appointed time; though it lingers, wait for it; it will surely come and will not delay. God’s words can survive time. Panic struggles to wait; manipulation needs instant compliance. Give it space. Many “urgent” impressions lose power after a night of sleep; God’s direction usually grows stronger, clearer, and more consistent over days and weeks. Doors will open or close. Provision or lack thereof will speak. Time doesn’t weaken God’s voice; it reveals it.

Let me weave in a simple psychological lens. Intrusive thoughts are often loud, repetitive, and driven by fear. They demand certainty now and punish you for not obeying their pressure. Values-led actions, on the other hand, are guided by what you deeply believe. As followers of Jesus, your values are clear: love God, love people, walk in truth, pursue holiness, do good, and trust Him. When you act from values—aligned with Scripture—you are more likely to be moving with God’s voice, even if you don’t feel sky-splitting assurance. Overthinking hunts certainty; faith walks in clarity and character.

Here’s a 60-second practice you can repeat any time. Pause and breathe. Name the thought out loud and write it down in one sentence so it stops looping in your head. Ask, is this like Jesus? Then open your Bible and find one clear passage that speaks to the principle involved, reading the surrounding context. Send a short text or voice note to a trusted mentor or friend who loves Scripture: Here’s what I’m sensing, here’s the verse, what do you see? Unless it is an obvious, immediate act of biblical obedience—like confessing a lie, forgiving someone, stopping a sinful behavior, showing mercy—wait twenty-four hours. See if peace settles or agitation grows. Let time and truth do their work.

If you fear missing God, hear this. A good Shepherd does not abandon sheep who wander; He rescues them. Psalm 32:8 says, I will instruct you and teach you in the way you should go; I will counsel you with My eye upon you. Romans 8:1 says there is no condemnation for those in Christ. God can redirect a willing heart. It’s far better to take a small, faithful step with humility than to freeze in fear waiting for 100 percent certainty. Many times the Lord’s guidance becomes clearest in motion, as you obey the light you have and keep listening.

Maybe right now you’re wrestling with a decision, and your chest is tight. Bring it under these five tests. Does it sound like the Shepherd? Does it align with Scripture? Does it produce pure, peaceable fruit? Does wise community confirm it? Can it stand the test of time? If the answer is yes, even if you feel a little shaky, take the step with Jesus. If the answer is unclear, wait with Him rather than rushing without Him. He is not late. He is loving you into wisdom.
