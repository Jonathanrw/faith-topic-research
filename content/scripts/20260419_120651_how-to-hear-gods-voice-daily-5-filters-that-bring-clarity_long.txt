Not sure if it’s God or your thoughts? Run it through these five filters. If you’ve been craving clarity in prayer, this simple, biblical framework can help you hear God’s voice daily with calm and confidence.

Many of us love God, but our minds are loud. Anxiety, fear, overthinking, and past wounds can drown out the whisper of the Shepherd. You ask, is this the Holy Spirit or just my brain spiraling? You’re not broken for wondering that. Jesus said, “My sheep hear my voice.” That means He expects you to hear, and He delights to guide you. Today, I want to give you five filters that bring clarity, plus a 60‑second discernment pause you can use anywhere, and a seven‑day journal practice to train your ears to His heart.

Filter one is Scripture alignment. God’s voice will never contradict God’s Word. Psalm 119 says His word is a lamp to our feet and a light to our path. Second Timothy says all Scripture is God‑breathed and useful for teaching and correcting. If an impression encourages bitterness, revenge, compromise, or despair, it is not the Lord. If your thought says, “You’re worthless. Give up,” that violates truth, because Romans 8 declares there is no condemnation for those in Christ, and Ephesians 2 calls you God’s workmanship. On the other hand, if you sense, “Confess and make it right,” or, “Take the humble path,” you’ll find those impulses echoed everywhere in Scripture. When in doubt, slow down and ask, where does the Bible speak to this? Even one verse can turn a foggy feeling into a clear next step. God’s Spirit loves to highlight God’s Word.

Filter two is Christlike fruit. The Spirit doesn’t just speak in words; He speaks in what He produces. Galatians 5 describes the fruit of the Spirit as love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self‑control. James 3 says the wisdom from above is pure, peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy and good fruit. So ask, if I follow this voice, will it grow love and humility? Will it cultivate peace that doesn’t need to control? Or will it fuel pride, panic, harshness, and isolation? Conviction from God is specific and hopeful: “Tell the truth. Return what you owe. Forgive as you’ve been forgiven.” Condemnation is vague and suffocating: “You always fail. Why try?” One leads you back to Jesus with courage; the other pushes you into shame and hiding. You can test any impression by its fruit.

Filter three is peace with conviction. Colossians 3 says, “Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts.” The word “rule” is like an umpire. Christ’s peace makes the call. That peace isn’t passive comfort or escapism. It is a settled, holy steadiness, often paired with a clear nudge. You may still feel butterflies, but under the nerves there’s an anchored yes. Sometimes the hard choice carries the deepest peace, because it’s aligned with God’s heart. Avoidance can feel like relief in the moment, but it erodes peace later. Jesus gives a peace that passes understanding, the kind that holds you even when circumstances don’t. When you sense a direction that is both calm and compelling, that’s a strong sign the Shepherd is leading.

Filter four is wise counsel. Proverbs teaches that in an abundance of counselors there is safety, and plans fail without guidance. God often confirms His voice through the body of Christ. Bring your sense to a mature believer who knows Scripture and knows you. Ask them to push back, ask questions, and pray. The goal is not to outsource obedience, but to submit your heart to community. The Spirit is not offended by accountability; He welcomes it. Watch out for echo chambers that only tell you what you want to hear, and be open to correction. God’s wisdom is “open to reason,” and humility keeps you safe.

Filter five is providence and fruit over time. Jesus said you will recognize things by their fruits. Not every open door is from God, and not every closed door is from the enemy. But over time, obedience produces character, grace in relationships, and faithful outcomes. If you sense God leading you to serve, forgive, build, or wait, and as you act you see increasing integrity, opportunities that don’t require you to compromise, and stories of grace in others, those are good fruits. If following a supposed word breeds secrecy, manipulation, or constant turmoil, step back and reassess. Give things time. God’s will is often confirmed in the slow growth of good fruit.

Here’s how to bring it all together with a 60‑second discernment pause you can use in a hallway, before a text, or during prayer. Set a one‑minute timer. Breathe in and pray, “Speak, Lord.” Breathe out, “Your servant is listening.” Ask quietly, does this align with Scripture I know? If not, stop there. If yes, would obeying this likely produce love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self‑control? Now check your heart: do I sense Christ’s peace with a clear nudge, or just panic and pressure? Picture one trusted, godly person. Would I gladly submit this to them for feedback? Finally, name one small, obedient step you can take today, then hold the rest open to watch for fruit over time. In sixty seconds, you’ve run the impression through all five filters without spiraling.

To train your ear day by day, try a simple seven‑day journal. Each day, write the date and the situation you’re praying about. Jot down what you sense God might be saying, even if it’s faint. Next to it, write at least one Scripture that speaks to it, and if you’re not sure, search a few verses and ask the Lord to highlight one. Note the likely fruit if you obey, and rate your peace on a scale of one to ten, noticing if it’s peace with conviction or just avoidance. Add the name of someone wise you will ask for counsel, and one small step of obedience you will take that day. At day’s end, record any fruit, doors that opened or closed, and how your heart changed. At the end of seven days, look back. You’ll start seeing patterns: God’s consistency, the Scriptures He keeps highlighting, the kinds of nudges that were truly from Him, and the counterfeit voices that kept you anxious. This is how you move from guessing to walking with God.

If God feels silent right now, remember that silence is not absence. Often He’s inviting you to slow down, open your Bible, and let His word become the tuning fork for your soul. Ask for wisdom; James 1 says God gives generously without finding fault. You are not too messy, too anxious, or too late to learn His voice. He is a Father who loves to speak, a Shepherd who knows your name, and a Counselor who leads you into peace.

So the next time a thought hits—send that message, take that job, end that habit, wait another day—run it through the five filters: Scripture, Christlike fruit, peace with conviction, wise counsel, and providence over time. Use the sixty‑second pause. Keep the seven‑day journal. Repeat. Over weeks and months, confusion shrinks and confidence grows, not because you got perfect, but because you grew close.
