If it’s God, it will pass these five tests. If you’ve been stuck asking, “Is this God speaking, or just my thoughts?” take a deep breath—there’s a simple, biblical way to know. You don’t have to stay paralyzed by overthinking or fear of getting it wrong. God is not trying to hide. He’s a good Shepherd, and He loves to lead you.

Today, I want to give you five clear filters from Scripture to help you recognize God’s voice versus your own thoughts. These are not magic tricks; they are steady guardrails that bring confidence. And because God is not the author of confusion, this will not only calm your anxiety, it will help you move forward with peace. Keep this video saved and come back to it whenever you’re discerning. Over time, you’ll build a history with God.

Test one is Scripture alignment. Second Timothy 3:16 says, “All Scripture is God-breathed and useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness.” God will never lead you in a way that contradicts His Word. If an impression urges you to lie, seek revenge, manipulate, or indulge what God calls sin, that’s not the Shepherd; that’s either flesh or the enemy. God’s voice may challenge you, but it won’t violate what He’s already spoken. Sometimes you’ll feel a nudge paired with a verse that gently surfaces, or a Bible passage you read that morning keeps echoing. Pay attention when the Spirit highlights Scripture in context. God’s voice is consistent with God’s book.

Test two is Christlike character. Jesus said in John 10:27, “My sheep hear my voice; I know them, and they follow me.” His voice carries His character. The Holy Spirit produces fruit—love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control, just like Galatians 5 describes. Ask, does this nudge move me toward Christlikeness? God’s voice convicts us specifically and offers a path forward; condemnation is vague, shaming, and hopeless. The Shepherd may say, “Apologize for those harsh words,” and give you courage to do it. The accuser says, “You’re a failure; why try?” One voice draws you to Jesus; the other drives you into despair. Follow the voice that forms Christ in you.

Test three is peace with clarity. Colossians 3:15 says, “Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts,” like an umpire calling balls and strikes. Sometimes we confuse adrenaline with anointing. God’s leading may stretch you, but underneath the nerves there will be a settled “yes” in your spirit, a clarity that doesn’t shout but stands. You might still feel butterflies, but the peace of Christ will be present—a peace that remains when you pray, when you pause, and when you picture obeying. This isn’t about choosing the easiest path; it’s about sensing God’s steady hand even when the path is difficult. If your “word” only increases chaos, panic, and pressure to rush, put it back on the altar and ask the Lord to rule with His peace.

Test four is wise counsel confirmation. Proverbs 11:14 says, “In an abundance of counselors there is safety.” God often confirms His leading through mature believers who love you, know Scripture, and will tell you the truth. Don’t cherry-pick people who will only agree with you. Share what you’re sensing with someone who will ask good questions, pray with you, and point you to Jesus. Counsel doesn’t replace God’s voice, but it tests your hearing. If multiple trusted voices independently echo the same caution or encouragement, pay attention. Humility will protect you here; pride makes us unteachable, but humility keeps our heart soft to God.

Test five is fruit over time. Jesus said, “You will know them by their fruits” in Matthew 7:16. Real guidance from God produces real fruit—not always instantly, but reliably. Look for what grows when you obey. Do you see increased love, deeper worship, more integrity, more freedom from sin, more courage to serve, more compassion for others? Or does it regularly produce confusion, secrecy, cutting corners, and eroding relationships? Time is your friend. If it’s God, it will bear good fruit in due season.

To help you walk this out, use a simple “voice log.” This is a quiet space where you track what you think God is saying and how it stands up to the tests. Write down the date and time, the situation you’re facing, the impression or thought you sensed, the Scripture that aligns with it, any counsel you sought and what they said, your peace level before and after prayer, the next small step of obedience you’ll take, and later, the fruit or outcome you notice over days and weeks. As you record, you’ll notice patterns. You’ll learn the tone of the Shepherd’s voice in your own story, and your confidence will grow.

Alongside the voice log, add a simple 24-hour wait rule. Unless the decision is a matter of clear obedience to a command of God or a true emergency, wait one full day before acting. Sleep on it. Let the adrenaline drop and the peace rise. Return to your notes in the morning. Pray, read Scripture, and run the five tests again. Impulsivity often dresses up like urgency. The Shepherd isn’t frantic. He leads beside still waters and makes a way in His time. Waiting 24 hours can save you from a lifetime of regret and will often turn a foggy impression into a clarified step.

Let me bring these five together in a real-life example. Imagine you sense, “Reach out and reconcile with that friend you hurt.” You take it to Scripture and remember Jesus’ words about leaving your gift at the altar to be reconciled. It aligns. You check the character test and admit this call moves you toward humility and love. You sit quietly and ask for peace, and though you feel nervous, you also sense a steady calm to write the message. You ask a wise believer if the timing seems right, and they encourage you with prayer and practical wisdom. You take the small step, and over time you see fruit—maybe the relationship heals, or maybe your own heart softens and obedience bears quiet joy. That is the Shepherd.

Now imagine a different scenario: “Quit everything today; God is done with you there.” You search Scripture and see God calling for faithfulness and wisdom. The character test reveals this thought is soaked in shame and resentment, not love or patience. Peace is absent; you feel frantic. Wise counsel urges you to slow down and make a plan. Over time, the fruit test shows the impulsive option would have caused unnecessary damage. The tests saved you from a counterfeit voice.

Please hear this: God is patient with learners. Even if you’ve missed it before, He knows how to find you again. A good Shepherd goes after wandering sheep. Jesus says in John 10, “My sheep hear my voice; I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and no one will snatch them out of my hand.” You are not hanging onto God by a thread; He is holding you by His hand. As you keep showing up with Scripture, seeking His character, guarding peace, inviting counsel, and watching for fruit, your discernment will sharpen. You’ll worry less and worship more.
