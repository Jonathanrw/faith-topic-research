If you’ve ever asked, “Is that God’s voice or just my thoughts?” this is for you. Before you act, run this 30‑second test: Source, Scripture, Fruit. It won’t make every decision easy, but it will make you steady.

Confusion about inner promptings fuels anxiety and indecision. You pray, you wait, and you wonder if you’re missing something. But God is not playing hide and seek with your heart. Jesus said, “My sheep hear my voice” in John 10:27. You were designed to discern, and with a simple, biblical grid you can move from panic to peace.

Here’s the test. First, Source: where is this coming from, and does it fit God’s character and wise counsel. Second, Scripture: does it align with the Bible. Third, Fruit: what is it producing in me and through me. Source. Scripture. Fruit. Thirty seconds. Let’s walk it through with real life.

Imagine this: late at night, your chest is tight, and you feel a sudden urge to send a harsh text to your ex or to your boss. “Just be done. Say it. Hit send now.” Source asks, who is leading me right now? The Shepherd or a stranger? God’s voice is consistent with His character—patient, truthful, loving, holy. The stranger’s voice is impulsive, chaotic, shaming. Proverbs 11:14 reminds us there is safety in an abundance of counselors. If the urge can’t stand five minutes of prayer or a quick text to a trusted, godly friend, it’s probably not the Shepherd. James 3:17 says the wisdom from above is pure, peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy and good fruit. If the voice in your head is closed to reason, demanding urgency without clarity, it’s not the wisdom from above.

Now Scripture. Second Timothy 3:16 says all Scripture is God‑breathed and useful for teaching, correcting, and training. God never leads you to contradict what He already revealed. If the thought says, “Lie to protect yourself,” Scripture says, “Speak the truth in love.” If the thought says, “Cut them down so you feel in control,” Scripture says, “Let no unwholesome talk come out of your mouth, but only what builds up.” Scripture also helps you separate conviction from condemnation. Condemnation says, “You’re a failure, beyond hope.” Conviction says, “That wasn’t right; come to Me and make it right.” The first pushes you away in shame; the second draws you near in repentance and hope. God’s voice may be firm, but it carries an open door.

Then Fruit. Galatians 5 describes the fruit of the Spirit: love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self‑control. James 3:17 adds purity, peace, reasonableness, mercy, sincerity. Ask, what is this producing in me and around me. Does this thought lead me toward peace and patience, or into panic and pressure. Does it move me to love or to bitterness. God’s promptings may stretch you, but they won’t strip you of the peace that comes from trusting Him.

Let’s try another scenario. You feel a nudge to apologize to someone you hurt. Source: Does this sound like the Shepherd, who leads toward humility and reconciliation, or like shame, which tells you to hide. Scripture: Jesus calls us to be reconciled quickly. That aligns. Fruit: You may feel nervous, but underneath is a steady sense of rightness, and afterward there’s peace. That’s the Fruit test in action.

Another one. You sense, “Quit your job tomorrow and step out in faith.” Sometimes God does lead boldly. But take the test. Source: is this an impulsive escape from discomfort, or a patient, prayerful leading that’s been developing over time. Scripture: God values work, provision, and caring for those entrusted to you. He leads in wisdom, not presumption. Fruit: does this thought create chronic turmoil and fear, or courageous peace. And remember counsel. Proverbs 11:14 again—wise guidance stabilizes you. Bring it to mature believers who love you and fear God, not just people who echo your preferences. If it’s God, wise counsel will not cancel His voice, but it will often clarify timing and next steps.

What about generosity. You feel a sudden urge to give money you had saved for something else. Source: is this manipulative guilt or cheerful prompting. Scripture: God loves a cheerful giver; He also calls us to provide for our families and not give under compulsion. Fruit: do you sense joy and peace to bless someone, or dread and resentment. If you’re unsure, take a day, pray, and ask counsel. God is not offended by your careful listening. He delights in it.

Now, here’s how to practice this so it becomes natural. Grab a journal. At the top of a page, write “Today’s Decision.” Under it, write what you think you’re hearing in one honest sentence. Then write three short lines: Source, Scripture, Fruit. After Source, write a few words about the tone of the thought and whether it fits God’s character and the input of wise counsel. After Scripture, write the verse or biblical principle it aligns with, or admit if you don’t know yet and plan to look it up. After Fruit, write the immediate effect this thought has on your heart and the likely impact on others. Under that, add “Next faithful step” and write one small, obedient action you can take today that doesn’t violate Scripture and moves you toward love. Finally, leave a little room to come back and record the outcome. Over time, this becomes your personal testimony of how God leads you.

If you struggle with overthinking, this template keeps you anchored. It takes the spinning thoughts from your head and puts them in the light with God. And it’s fast. In thirty seconds you can ask, Source, Scripture, Fruit, and sense whether to pause or proceed. If you need more time, take it. Waiting on God is not wasting time. Often the waiting itself is how He turns up the volume on His peace and turns down the noise of fear.

A quick word about silence. If you run the test and still feel unsure, remember this: God’s silence is not absence. Sometimes He withholds specifics because the next step is simply to be faithful where you are. Keep doing the last thing He clearly asked you to do. Stay in the Word. Stay in community. Keep your heart soft. The Shepherd knows how to get through to His sheep when it’s time to move. You don’t have to force it.

And a word about grace. You will not ruin God’s plan by a sincere mistake. If you step thinking it was God and later realize it wasn’t, He knows how to reroute a willing heart. Grace does not make you careless; it makes you courageous. You can walk, learn, and adjust without living in fear.

So next time you wonder, Is that God or my thoughts, remember this simple grid. Source: does this sound like the Shepherd and stand up to wise counsel. Scripture: does it align with God’s Word. Fruit: does it produce love, joy, peace, and wisdom. When those three line up, you can move forward with a quiet confidence. When they don’t, you can wait without shame.
