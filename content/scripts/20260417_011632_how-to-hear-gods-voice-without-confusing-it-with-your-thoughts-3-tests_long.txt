Think you heard God — but not sure? Try these three biblical tests before you act.

A few years ago a woman I know felt a clear, urgent impression to quit her job and move cities. At the same time she had fear, excitement, and a little voice telling her to wait. She prayed, she Googled, she replayed the thought over and over until she couldn't tell which voice was God, which was hope, and which was simply fear. Maybe that sounds familiar. We all face moments like that — decisions laced with competing inner voices. The stakes feel high, and the last thing you want is to follow your own wishful thinking and call it a word from the Lord.

Here’s the good news: God doesn’t leave us to a guessing game. The Bible gives simple, reliable tests to help us discern His voice from our thoughts. They aren’t mystical tricks. They are practical, biblical guardrails you can use today. I want to walk you through three of them: Scripture alignment, peace and fruit over time, and wise community confirmation. I’ll show you how they work, give brief examples, warn you about common pitfalls, and end with a short prayer you can use right now.

First, the Scripture test. If it’s truly God speaking, it will never contradict Scripture. God’s voice will agree with His character and His revealed will. Scripture is the measuring rod. If an impression tells you to do something that conflicts with God’s commands — to lie, to exploit someone, or to neglect love and justice — that impression is not from God. For example, a man felt compelled to get revenge after being wronged. The sense of “justice now” felt intense, but the Bible calls us to leave vengeance to God and to pursue reconciliation when possible. So the impression failed the Scripture test.

Pitfall: emotional intensity can masquerade as divine certainty. Just because a thought feels powerful doesn’t make it biblical. Make a habit of asking, “Does this align with Scripture?” If you’re unsure, look up passages about God’s character and relevant commands. Memorize a few key verses like Philippians 4:6–7 about prayer and peace, or Galatians 5:22–23 about the fruit of the Spirit. Scripture is the foundation; if an impression stands on that ground, it’s worth further testing.

Second, the peace and fruit test. Jesus said, “My sheep hear my voice” and they know it by the Shepherd’s presence. God gives a sense of peace, not ongoing torment, and His leading produces fruit consistent with His nature over time. That doesn’t mean you won’t feel nervous — big steps can be scary — but the overall trajectory is marked by spiritual fruit: humility, love, patience, self-control, greater dependence on God. If a decision produces anxiety, increased selfishness, or spiritual dryness that worsens over time, that’s a red flag.

Here’s an example: a woman sensed a prompting to cut off every friend who disappointed her. Initially it felt cleansing, even liberating. But after acting, she felt more isolated, proud, and more easily offended. The decision produced bad fruit. The test exposed the source. Sometimes we confuse relief or temporary adrenaline with God’s peace. Watch the fruit over weeks and months. God’s voice leads to life, not further spiritual decay.

Pitfall: waiting for an immediate, cinematic “peace” moment. Discernment often unfolds slowly. Give it time. Small tests and incremental steps let you observe the fruit without leaping into irreversible choices.

Third, the community confirmation test. God rarely speaks only to your heart and not through His people. Scripture values counsel — Proverbs calls many advisers wise, and the early church tested prophecies in community. Bring your impression to trusted, mature believers: a pastor, mentor, or spiritually wise friend. Share what you sense, explain how it aligns with Scripture, and listen humbly to their observations. They may bring Scripture you missed, see blind spots, or confirm a clear lead.

Example: a young man felt called to a ministry but was tempted to hurry and fundraise alone. His pastor and a couple of mature friends saw practical obstacles and spiritual immaturity; they asked hard questions that helped him realize he needed more training. Their counsel didn’t silence God; it refined his obedience. Community doesn’t replace God’s voice, but it helps interpret it.

Pitfall: seeking only affirmation. If you gather friends who always agree, you’ll have an echo chamber. Invite honest, loving critique and be willing to wait for wise consensus.

So how do you put these tests into practice right now? Start with a simple prayer: “Lord, if this is from you, show me through your Word, give me your peace, and lead me to wise counsel. Don’t let me mistake my desires for your voice.” Then do the homework. Look up relevant Scripture; journal what you sense and how it aligns. Take a small, reversible step if possible and watch the fruit. Share the impression with one or two trusted believers and ask them to pray and speak honestly. If three tests point in the same direction — Scripture, peace/fruit, and community — you can move forward with confidence. If they don’t line up, be patient. Discernment often grows through waiting and refining.

Remember, discernment is not a single miraculous event but a skill formed over time. If you’re worried you’ll never get it right, lean on God’s patience. He trains us like a loving parent, giving correction, blessings, and course adjustments until we learn to recognize His voice more clearly. The more you root your decisions in Scripture, observe spiritual fruit, and walk with wise community, the calmer and clearer your discernment will become.
